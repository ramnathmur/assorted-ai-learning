Pretraining data — the internet

Entry

You'll see exactly what "the model was trained on the internet" actually means — what gets pulled in, what gets thrown out, and why quality filtering matters more than raw volume past a point.

So let's start at the beginning, which is: where does the data come from? When people say "ChatGPT was trained on the internet," it sounds like a slogan. But there's a real, concrete, surprisingly grubby pipeline behind that phrase, and I want to walk you through it because once you've seen it, a lot of model behavior makes more sense.srctranscript 01:02

The place I usually point people is the FineWeb blog post on HuggingFace. FineWeb is an open dataset put together by the HuggingFace team that mirrors, more or less, what the frontier labs do behind closed doors. Their blog post is genuinely fantastic — it walks through every stage of the pipeline, shows you the numbers shrinking at each step, and explains why each filter exists.srcHuggingFace FineWeb blog post — Penedo et al. 2024

The raw input is something called Common Crawl. Common Crawl is a non-profit that's been crawling the web since 2007. Cumulatively across all its snapshots, the archive has captured hundreds of billions of web pages (~250B+ by recent counts), totaling several hundred terabytes (petabytes, in aggregate) of HTML. Every month they release a new snapshot containing billions of fresh pages. This is the starting point for almost every pretraining dataset you've ever heard of — C4, The Pile, RefinedWeb, FineWeb, all of them.srcCommon Crawl official site, commoncrawl.org

Now, you can't just dump Common Crawl into a model. If you did, you'd be training your model on, among other things, an astonishing amount of garbage — boilerplate, malware sites, spam, racism, adult content, and just plain duplicated text repeated thousands of times. So there's a pipeline of filters, and each one of them throws away a huge fraction of what you started with.srctranscript 01:02

The first filter is URL filtering. You maintain a giant blocklist of domains you don't want — adult sites, malware-distributing sites, racist forums, that kind of thing. Common Crawl just crawled whatever it could reach; you have to be the one deciding what your model should never see. The blocklists are public; you can go look at them.srcHuggingFace FineWeb blog post

The second step is text extraction. A web page is HTML — tags, CSS, JavaScript, navigation bars, footers, ads, cookie banners. What you actually want is the prose in the middle. So you run an extractor that tries to strip the chrome and keep the content. This is a surprisingly hard problem; whole research papers exist on doing it well. The output is something that looks like clean markdown-ish text.srcHuggingFace FineWeb blog post

Third, language filtering. Most pretraining datasets keep one language, or a handful — English dominates, but you can imagine training a French-first or Chinese-first model. You run a language classifier on each page and keep only the ones that match. After this filter, the dataset is much smaller because — surprise — a huge fraction of the web is not in English.srcHuggingFace FineWeb blog post

Fourth, deduplication. The web has an absurd amount of near-duplicate content. Articles get copied across mirrors. Boilerplate text — terms of service, copyright notices, cookie warnings — appears on millions of pages. If you don't dedupe, you'll train your model to memorize boilerplate, and you'll waste compute on text it's seen a thousand times. Dedup uses techniques like MinHash and locality-sensitive hashing — we won't go into the math, but the point is: it kills duplicates aggressively.srcHuggingFace FineWeb blog post

Fifth, PII removal. Personally identifiable information — phone numbers, email addresses, social security numbers — gets scrubbed or replaced. You don't want the model to memorize and regurgitate someone's email or SSN. The scrubbing is heuristic and imperfect, but it catches the obvious cases.srcHuggingFace FineWeb blog post

After all of that, what comes out the other end is the actual pretraining corpus. For FineWeb specifically, the number is around 15 trillion tokens of clean text, which translates to roughly 44 terabytes on disk. To put that in perspective: if you were to print 15 trillion tokens at typical book density, you'd have a stack of books reaching to the moon and back many times. It's a lot of text. But it's the distilled output of a much, much larger raw pile.srcHuggingFace FineWeb blog post

And here's the thing that surprises people: past a certain point, more data doesn't help nearly as much as better data. The Chinchilla scaling laws taught us that you want roughly 20 tokens per parameter, but more recent work has shown that you can keep training way past that as long as the data quality stays high. The frontier labs spend enormous effort on filtering, weighting, and curating — far more than they spend on getting more raw text. The signal-to-noise ratio of your corpus is the lever.srctranscript 01:02

The shape of the data shapes the model. Every fact, style, and code snippet in the weights came from this pile. If the corpus doesn't contain a fact, the model doesn't know it.

Let me give you one more way to think about this. The pretraining corpus is, in a real sense, what the model knows. Every fact, every writing style, every code snippet, every Wikipedia article that gets distilled into the weights — it all came from this pile of text. If FineWeb (or its closed-source equivalent) doesn't contain a fact, the model doesn't know it. If a topic is overrepresented in the corpus (let's say JavaScript tutorials), the model will be way better at it than topics that are underrepresented (let's say Brazilian Portuguese poetry from the 1930s).srctranscript 01:02

And what's really interesting, and we'll come back to this in Section 11, is that this is also where hallucinations come from. The model has a lossy compressed version of all this text inside its weights. When you ask it about something it half-knows, it'll cheerfully make up the rest, because the loss is smoothed out — there's no sharp boundary between "I memorized this" and "I have no idea." We'll get into that.srctranscript 01:02

A few things are excluded on purpose. Most labs strip out copyrighted books and code where they can identify it (though there's always leakage). Many strip out very recent content past a "training cutoff" date — that's why the model often doesn't know about events from the last few months. Some explicitly add high-quality curated sources like Wikipedia, arXiv papers, GitHub code — those get up-weighted, sometimes seen multiple times during training. The exact recipe is a closely guarded trade secret, and each lab's recipe is a little different.srcHuggingFace FineWeb blog post; transcript 01:02

So the picture I want you to leave this section with: the pretraining corpus is a carefully filtered slice of the public internet, on the order of tens of trillions of tokens, dominated by English, scrubbed for PII, deduplicated aggressively, with high-quality sources up-weighted. It's not "the internet" — it's a curated, opinionated reduction of the internet. And every choice in that reduction shapes what the model knows.srctranscript 01:02

Tokenization

Entry

You'll see how text gets converted into integers — the units the model actually consumes — and why this seemingly boring detail is the source of half the most famous LLM failures.

Okay so here's a thing that's going to seem like a technicality and then end up haunting us for the rest of the course. Neural networks don't read text. They read numbers. Specifically, they read a sequence of integers, where each integer is an index into a fixed vocabulary. So before we can train on FineWeb, we have to convert every piece of text into a sequence of integers. That conversion is called tokenization, and the tool that does it is called a tokenizer.srctranscript 07:47

Now, the dumb way to do this would be one integer per character. You'd have a vocabulary of about 128 tokens (for ASCII) and every word would just become a sequence of character indices. The problem is sequences would be enormous and the model would have to learn from scratch that "the" appears all the time. So nobody does that.srctranscript 07:47

The other dumb extreme would be one integer per word. But then "running," "runs," "ran," and "run" all become unrelated tokens, and you have to handle every misspelling and new word that ever shows up. Also nobody does that.srctranscript 07:47

What everyone actually does is a middle path called byte-pair encoding, or BPE. The idea is beautifully simple: start with characters, then iteratively merge the most frequent adjacent pair into a new token, and repeat. You end up with a vocabulary that contains common words as single tokens, common subword pieces ("ing", "tion", "the"), and falls back to characters for rare strings.srcSennrich et al. 2016, "Neural Machine Translation of Rare Words with Subword Units"

Let me show you what this looks like in practice. There's a beautiful little web tool called Tiktokenizer at tiktokenizer.vercel.app. You paste text in, you pick a model, and it shows you exactly how that text gets chopped up and what the integer IDs are. I want you to actually go play with it — there are things you'll notice that I couldn't tell you in a paragraph.srctiktokenizer.vercel.app

For example: paste in "hello world" and you'll see it become something like two tokens — hello and world (with a leading space). Note that the space is part of the next token, not its own token. This is a quirk of GPT-style tokenizers. Now paste in just "hello" by itself versus " hello" with a leading space — you'll get different token IDs. The model sees these as related but distinct. This is the kind of thing that makes prompt engineering a little weird; tiny formatting differences become different token sequences.srctranscript 07:47

A few numbers to anchor you. GPT-2 had a vocabulary of about 50,257 tokens. GPT-4 has about 100,277. Llama 3 uses about 128,000. The vocabulary size is a design choice — bigger vocab means shorter sequences (good) but more parameters in the embedding and output layers (more compute). The trend over time has been to grow the vocabulary as models get bigger; it pays for itself.srctranscript 07:47

There's also a thing called special tokens that get added to the vocabulary by hand. These aren't learned from text — they're inserted by the people building the model to mark structural things. Tokens like <|im_start|>, <|im_end|>, <|system|> are used to delimit roles in a chat conversation (we'll see this in detail in Section 10). They're not text — they're more like control codes.srctranscript 07:47

Mental model #1 · The token tumbler

Here's a thing I want to plant in your head now and cash in later: the model never sees letters. It sees integer token IDs. When you ask "how many Rs are in strawberry?", the model isn't seeing s-t-r-a-w-b-e-r-r-y the way you do — it's seeing maybe two tokens like straw and berry. The R-level structure is invisible to it.

We'll come back to this in Section 14 — it's why models are weirdly bad at character-counting and string reversal. The model sees a tumbled-up integer representation, never the raw stuff.

A couple of practical things before we move on. When people quote a "context length of 8,000" or "128,000", that's the number of tokens, not words or characters. As a very rough rule of thumb, in English, 1 token ≈ 0.75 words ≈ 4 characters. So an 8K context is roughly 6,000 words, and 128K is roughly 96,000 words — about a full novel. But this varies a lot by language and content type — code, math, and non-English languages tokenize very differently.srctranscript 07:47

If you want to go deeper on the actual algorithm, I wrote a small repo called minbpe that implements BPE in about 600 lines of Python. It's deliberately minimal and readable. The original BPE paper by Sennrich, Haddow, and Birch (2016) is also a great short read — they invented BPE for machine translation; LLMs picked it up later.srcKarpathy's minbpe GitHub repo; Sennrich et al. 2016

So zoom out. We started with FineWeb's 15 trillion tokens of clean text. Step zero of training is to run all of it through the tokenizer, converting every document into a sequence of integers from the vocabulary. Now we have 15 trillion integer IDs sitting on disk, ready to be fed to a neural network. That neural network is what we'll look at next.srctranscript 07:47

Neural network I/O

Entry

Forget the internals for a second — we'll just nail down what goes into the network (a window of tokens) and what comes out (a probability distribution over the next token). The contract.

So before we open the hood and look at the transformer guts, I want to nail down the I/O contract. Like, what is the actual interface? What does the neural network take in, and what does it produce? Because if you have this picture clear, everything else makes more sense.srctranscript 14:28

Here's the contract. The input is: a sequence of token IDs, of some bounded length. The output is: a probability distribution over the next token. That's it. That's the whole black box, at the interface level.srctranscript 14:28

The bounded length is what we call the context window. Different models have different limits. GPT-2 had 1024 tokens. GPT-4 originally had 8,000 (often written as "8K"). Modern models — Gemini 1.5, Claude 3.5, GPT-4 Turbo — have pushed into 128,000 (128K) or even 1 million or more. The number you pick is a tradeoff: longer context lets the model see more, but the compute cost grows steeply with sequence length (roughly quadratically with vanilla attention), and the model has to learn to handle longer dependencies.srctranscript 14:28

When you feed the model a sequence of, say, 500 tokens, it looks at all 500 at once (in parallel — this is one of the reasons transformers are fast on GPUs) and produces one probability distribution for the next token. At inference, what you care about is the very last position's distribution.srctranscript 14:28

Now, the distribution is over the entire vocabulary. So if the vocabulary is 100,000 tokens, the output is a vector of 100,000 probabilities, all summing to 1. Most of those probabilities are tiny — close to zero. A few are noticeable. Often one or two are dominant. The shape of that distribution carries a lot of information: a sharp peak means the model is confident; a flat distribution means it has no strong prior.srctranscript 14:28

Let me show you what I mean. Suppose the model has just seen the tokens for "The Eiffel Tower is in". The next-token distribution is going to put a lot of mass on Paris. Maybe 0.85. Then France might be 0.05. The rest is scattered noise. The model has learned, from billions of internet sentences, that "The Eiffel Tower is in Paris" is the way that sentence almost always continues.srctranscript 14:28

But now suppose the prompt is "I like to eat". The distribution is going to be much flatter — lots of foods, lots of possibilities. pizza, ice, chicken, lunch — all reasonable. None of them dominant. That's a high-entropy distribution, and it's where sampling decisions (which we'll see in Section 6) really matter.srctranscript 14:28

Now, the I/O contract has a few non-obvious consequences. Let me draw out three.srctranscript 14:28

  1. The model has no memory across calls. If you send a prompt, get a response, and then send another prompt, the second call doesn't "remember" the first. Each forward pass starts from scratch with whatever tokens you put in the context window. What feels like a conversation in ChatGPT is achieved by the system concatenating the entire conversation history and sending it as the prompt every single turn. (See Section 10.)srctranscript 14:28
  2. The model's knowledge is in the parameters, but accessed via the context window. The parameters store a vast, lossy, compressed memory of pretraining. The context window is sharp, short-term, in-the-moment. Two completely different memory systems. What you put in the context window is exact; what the model "knows" from training is approximate. (See Section 11.)srctranscript 14:28
  3. The output distribution is over tokens, not words or concepts. Word boundaries, sentence boundaries, paragraph structure — those all emerge from the model's learned probabilities, not from any structural rule.srctranscript 14:28

That last point is the bridge to the next section. The model is doing one forward pass per token. The way you generate a long response is by running the forward pass over and over, sampling a new token each time, and appending. That's the inference loop, and that's where we go next.srctranscript 14:28

If you want depth on what's inside, the canonical reference is Vaswani et al. 2017, "Attention is All You Need" — the paper that introduced the transformer. We're not going to derive any of it. For a beautiful visual treatment, see the bbycroft.net/llm visualizer (pointed at in the next section), the Illustrated Transformer by Jay Alammar, and 3Blue1Brown's transformer chapter.srcVaswani et al. 2017; bbycroft.net/llm; Jay Alammar's Illustrated Transformer; 3Blue1Brown transformer series 2024

Neural network internals

Entry

A deliberately shallow tour of what's inside the transformer — enough to put real numbers on "parameters" and "layers" without doing any math.

I'm going to keep this section short and deliberately hand-wavy, because the goal of this course is not to teach you how to derive a transformer. There are great resources for that. The goal here is to give you the right mental picture so that when you hear "405 billion parameters" you have a sense of what those parameters are, and when you hear "transformer block" you know what's in it.srctranscript 20:11

A transformer is, structurally, very repetitive. It's a stack of identical building blocks, called transformer blocks or sometimes layers. Each block has the same shape — two main pieces inside: an attention sub-layer and an MLP (multi-layer perceptron) sub-layer. The attention sub-layer is where tokens can "talk to" each other — it's where information flows across the sequence. The MLP sub-layer is where individual token representations get transformed in a more standalone way. Then there are some normalization and residual-connection tricks holding it all together, but the high-level picture is: attention, MLP, attention, MLP, repeated dozens of times.srcVaswani et al. 2017; Karpathy "Let's build GPT from scratch"

Llama 3 405B has 126 of these blocks stacked. GPT-3 had 96. GPT-2 small had 12. The number of blocks (the depth) and the size of the vectors that flow through them (the width) are the two main knobs you turn to make the model bigger.srcDubey et al. 2024 "The Llama 3 Herd of Models"; Radford et al. 2019 GPT-2 paper

Now, parameters. Every layer has weights — matrices of floating-point numbers — that get learned during training. The word "parameters" usually refers to all of these weights together. When someone says "Llama 3 has 405 billion parameters," they mean there are 405,000,000,000 individual floating-point numbers that were adjusted during training. Each one is, in some sense, a single tunable knob.srcDubey et al. 2024; transcript 20:11

The way I want you to picture this: imagine an enormous mixing console with 405 billion sliders. Training is the process of nudging each slider very slightly, billions of times, until the model gets good at predicting next tokens. Once training is done, you freeze all the sliders, and inference is just the math of running data through that fixed configuration.srctranscript 20:11

The unsettling part

Here's the thing that's both magical and a bit unsettling: nobody — not the people who trained Llama 3, not the people who trained GPT-4, nobody — knows what most of those parameters individually do. They learn what they learn. We can probe some of them (there's a research field called mechanistic interpretability making progress), but in general, the model is a black box at the parameter level. We know how it was trained, what objective it optimized, and its overall behavior empirically — but the specific role of any given weight is mostly unknown.

Okay, so where can you go look at this? Brendan Bycroft built an amazing 3D visualizer of a small GPT at bbycroft.net/llm. I cannot recommend it enough — go open it in another tab right now if you have a minute.srcbbycroft.net/llm — Brendan Bycroft's GPT visualizer

Jay Alammar's "Illustrated Transformer" is the classic 2D introduction. 3Blue1Brown's transformer chapters (2024) are the best math-visual explanations I've ever seen. Any of those will give you mechanism — what I'm giving you here is just enough mechanism to not be afraid of the words.srcJay Alammar "The Illustrated Transformer"; 3Blue1Brown transformer series 2024; Karpathy "Let's build GPT from scratch"

Pretraining · §6 · transcript 26:01

Inference

Entry

You'll see how the same trained model gets called over and over in a loop to produce a paragraph, and why two runs of "the same prompt" can give you different outputs.

So we have this trained box. It takes tokens in and gives a probability distribution out. The question is: how do you go from that single forward pass to a multi-paragraph response? The answer is the inference loop, and it's beautifully simple once you see it.srctranscript 26:01

Here's the loop. You start with a prompt — let's say "Once upon a time". You tokenize it. You feed the token sequence into the model. You get a probability distribution over the next token. You sample one token from that distribution. You append the sampled token to your sequence. Now you have a slightly longer sequence. You feed that back into the model. You get a new distribution. You sample again. Append. Repeat.srctranscript 26:01

That's it. That's text generation. Sample, append, re-feed, repeat. Every word you've ever read from ChatGPT was produced one token at a time by this loop.

The word sample is doing a lot of work here. The model doesn't pick the most probable token automatically. If it did, every call with the same prompt would produce the exact same output, which is not what you want. Instead, you draw a token probabilistically from the distribution. So if the distribution says there is 0.31, you'll pick there about 31% of the time and something else 69% of the time.srctranscript 26:01

This is why the model is stochastic, not deterministic, by design. Two runs of the same prompt will likely give you different outputs, because the random sampling will pick different tokens at different positions. That's a feature, not a bug — it gives you variety and creative ability. (For some applications you might want determinism; you can either set the random seed or use a very low "temperature" setting.)srctranscript 26:01

Now let me introduce three knobs you can turn to shape the sampling. These are the parameters you'll see in any LLM API.srctranscript 26:01

Temperature. A knob between 0 and roughly 2. Low temperature (near 0) makes the distribution sharper — the most probable tokens become almost certain. High temperature flattens it — even less-probable tokens get a real shot. Temperature 1.0 uses the raw probabilities as-is; 0 collapses to always picking the single most probable token (deterministic but boring). Creative writing: 0.8–1.0. Factual Q&A: 0.2.srcHugging Face "How to generate text" — Patrick von Platen 2020

Top-k. Instead of sampling from the full distribution over 100,000 tokens, restrict it to only the top k most probable ones. Common values are k=40 or k=50. This prevents the rare cases where the model accidentally samples a really weird, low-probability token.srcHugging Face "How to generate text"

Top-p (nucleus sampling). Smarter than a fixed k — take the smallest set of top tokens whose cumulative probability exceeds p. So p=0.9 means "sample from however many top tokens it takes to cover 90% of the probability mass." Top-p adapts to the entropy: if the model is confident, maybe 2 tokens cover 90%; if unsure, maybe 50 do.srcHoltzman et al. 2019 "The Curious Case of Neural Text Degeneration"

Reinforcement learning from human feedback (RLHF)

Entry

This is the section that demystifies the most-talked-about technique in modern LLMs. You'll see how RLHF actually works, why it's not the same kind of RL as AlphaGo, and why Karpathy says "RLHF is barely RL."

So we now know that for verifiable problems (math, code, games), RL can transcend humans. But what about the vast majority of what we ask LLMs for? Writing a poem. Summarizing an article. Telling a joke. Being helpful in a conversation. There's no verifier for "is this a good joke?" — you can't programmatically check it. This is the problem RLHF is designed to solve. It's worth understanding precisely because (a) it's how most production LLMs get their final polish, and (b) it has very different properties from AlphaGo-style RL.srctranscript 2:48:26

The setup, in three steps. Step 1: Collect human preferences. Take a prompt, generate two candidate responses, show them to a human labeler, ask "which is better?" Record: prompt → (winner, loser). Do this thousands of times.srcChristiano et al. 2017 "Deep Reinforcement Learning from Human Preferences"; Ouyang et al. 2022

Step 2: Train a reward model. Take the pairwise data and train a separate neural network — the reward model — to predict, for any (prompt, response) pair, a scalar "how much would a human prefer this?" It's trained so winners score higher than losers, and afterward it can score any new response. Structurally it's another LLM — prompt + response in, scalar out — whose only job is to imitate the average human labeler. Step 3: RL the LLM against the reward model. Treat the reward model as your verifier and run the standard RL loop: sample candidates, score with the reward model, upweight high scorers. Looks just like Section 17's loop, except the verifier is now a learned imitation of human preferences rather than ground truth.srcChristiano et al. 2017; Ouyang et al. 2022

For a concrete example, take "Tell me a joke about pelicans." The LLM generates 16 candidates. The reward model scores each. High scorers get upweighted, low scorers downweighted. After many iterations, the LLM is more likely to produce jokes the reward model scores highly — which, by transitivity, are jokes humans tend to prefer.srctranscript 2:48:26

Now let me tell you the three things that make RLHF fragile and structurally different from AlphaGo. Fragility 1: The reward model has a ceiling. The best it can do is accurately imitate the labelers. So the LLM can at best converge to "what the average labeler thinks is good." You cannot transcend with RLHF. This is fundamentally unlike AlphaGo, where the verifier was the actual game outcome.srctranscript 2:48:26

Fragility 2: Reward hacking. Because the reward model is a fallible neural net, there are inputs (often nonsensical-looking) it scores very high even though no human would. And RL is adversarial — it explicitly searches for inputs that maximize the reward model's score. So given enough training, the LLM discovers these adversarial inputs and produces high-scoring gibberish. The canonical example: the "the the the the" failure — researchers ran RLHF too aggressively and the model started producing the word "the" repeated, which the reward model scored near-maximum.srcGao et al. 2022 "Scaling Laws for Reward Model Overoptimization"

The fix in practice is early stopping — stop RL before the model overfits to the reward model. You also use KL penalties (keeping the new model close to the original SFT model), but the core defense is: don't run RLHF too long. There's a sweet spot, and going past it makes things worse. Fragility 3: It's a small nudge, not a leap. Because of the ceiling and the reward-hacking risk, you can only do so much RLHF. It shifts a SFT model modestly toward "more preferred by humans" — it doesn't fundamentally change what the model can do; it polishes how it does it.srcGao et al. 2022; transcript 2:48:26

This is why Karpathy uses the phrase: "RLHF is barely RL." The form is RL — sample, score, update — but the content is closer to an extended fine-tuning step with a fancy loss function. No transcendence. No move 37.

  2. 2. Vague recollection vs working memory. Parameters hold a lossy, smeared memory of pretraining; the context window holds sharp, exact, current information. Hallucinations come from over-relying on the former; tool use moves answers from the former to the latter.srctranscript 3:21:46
  3. 3. Three-stage pipeline. Pretraining for knowledge, SFT for behavior, RL for reasoning. Each uses radically different data and compute. Everything you read about LLMs sits somewhere on this conveyor.srctranscript 3:21:46
  4. 4. Models need tokens to think. Each forward pass is a fixed compute budget; longer answers literally allow more thinking. Show-your-work isn't style — it's how the model reasons. Reasoning models scale by writing more.srctranscript 3:21:46
  5. 5. Statistical simulation of a labeler. What feels like "an AI" is a learned imitation of a contractor pool — then polished by RL. ChatGPT's tics, refusals, and tone are choices made by labelers, distilled into the weights.srctranscript 3:21:46
  6. 6. Jagged intelligence. Superhuman on some tasks, sub-elementary on others, with sharp edges that don't show until you probe. Don't generalize too far from one positive — or negative — result.srctranscript 3:21:46
  7. 7. RLHF is not real RL. RLHF is a capped, fragile polish using a learned reward model. AlphaGo-style verifier-based RL is the real thing and can transcend humans on verifiable domains. Same machinery, different properties.srctranscript 3:21:46
  8. 8. Move 37. RL with a clean verifier can discover strategies no human would teach. SFT cannot. This is why reasoning models matter — and why labeler quality is a ceiling, not a floor.srctranscript 3:21:46

Reference · Glossary

Glossary

A quick-reference set of the key terms used across the course. Alphabetical, one-sentence definitions, each tagged with where it's introduced.

Activation
The output values flowing through a neural network layer at inference, as opposed to the learned weights (parameters). src§5
Attention
The sub-layer in a transformer block that lets tokens in a sequence "talk to" each other; the mechanism enabling long-range dependencies. srcVaswani et al. 2017
Autoregressive
A generation pattern where the model produces output one token at a time, feeding each generated token back as input for the next step. src§6
Backward pass
The training phase where the model computes how to nudge each parameter to reduce loss (backpropagation). src§7
Base model
The pure pretrained model, before any SFT or RL; behaves like an internet-document completer rather than an assistant. src§8
Batch
A group of training sequences processed together in parallel to amortize compute. src§7
Byte-pair encoding (BPE)
The dominant tokenization algorithm: starting from characters, iteratively merge the most frequent adjacent pair into new tokens. srcSennrich et al. 2016
Chain of thought (CoT)
A prompting/training pattern where the model writes intermediate reasoning before the final answer; works because each step is a forward pass of compute. srcWei et al. 2022
Chat template
The structured format (special tokens + role markers) that encodes a multi-turn conversation as one token sequence. src§10
Context window
The maximum number of tokens the model can see at once; the bound on its "working memory." src§4
Cross-entropy
The loss function used in pretraining; penalizes the model for assigning low probability to the correct next token. src§7
Distillation
Training a smaller model to imitate a larger model's outputs, often yielding a fast, cheap model that retains much of the parent's capability. srcgeneral AI literature
Early stopping
Halting RLHF training before the policy overfits the reward model and starts reward-hacking. src§20
Elo
The chess rating system, used by LMSYS Chatbot Arena to rank LLMs from pairwise blind preference votes. srclmarena.ai
Embedding
The vector representation of a token used internally; the first thing that happens to token IDs entering the transformer. src§5
Epoch
One full pass through the training data; modern LLM training typically does less than one epoch on a very large corpus. src§7
Fine-tuning
Continuing to train a pretrained model on a new dataset to shift its behavior or knowledge. src§9
Forward pass
One run of input tokens through the model to produce a next-token distribution. src§4
Function calling
A model's structured ability to invoke external tools (search, code interpreter, APIs); same concept as "tool use." srcSchick et al. 2023
Gradient
The direction in parameter space that reduces loss; what backpropagation computes. src§7
Hallucination
The model confidently generating plausible-sounding content with no basis in fact, typically when its parametric memory is vague. src§11
Instruct model
A model that has been through SFT (and usually RLHF) to behave as a conversational assistant. src§8
Jagged intelligence
The property that LLM ability is unevenly distributed across tasks — superhuman on some, sub-elementary on others, in unpredictable patches. src§15
KL penalty
A regularization term that keeps the RL policy close to the SFT model, mitigating reward hacking. src§20
Leaderboard
A public ranking of LLMs on benchmarks or human preferences (e.g., LMSYS Arena, Artificial Analysis). src§22
Learning rate
A hyperparameter controlling the size of each parameter update during training. src§7
Logit
The raw pre-softmax score the model assigns to each vocabulary token at a given position. src§4
Loss
The numerical measure of how wrong the model's predictions are; what training minimizes. src§7
MoE (Mixture of Experts)
An architecture where many specialized sub-networks exist and only a few are active per token, allowing larger total parameter counts at fixed inference cost. srcgeneral AI literature
Open weights
A model whose trained parameters are released publicly, even if the training data and code are not (distinct from fully open-source). src§22
Optimizer
The algorithm (AdamW, etc.) that applies gradients to update parameters during training. src§7
Pairwise preference
The basic unit of RLHF data: two candidate responses to the same prompt with a human's choice between them. src§20
Parameter
One of the billions of learned floating-point numbers in a neural network; collectively they store everything the model learned. src§5
Parametric memory
Knowledge stored in the weights — vague, lossy, prone to hallucination — contrasted with contextual memory in the context window. src§11
Policy
In RL, the function (here, the LLM) that produces actions (here, tokens) given states (here, contexts). src§17
Pretraining
The first and most expensive training stage: predicting next tokens over tens of trillions of internet tokens. src§2–7
Probability distribution
The output of one forward pass: a probability for each token in the vocabulary, summing to 1. src§4
Quantization
Compressing weights from 16- or 32-bit floats to lower precision (8-bit, 4-bit) to reduce memory and speed up inference. srcgeneral AI literature
Reasoning model
A model trained with verifier-based RL to do long internal monologue before answering; o1, DeepSeek-R1, Gemini thinking models. src§18
Reward
In RL, the scalar signal indicating how good an output was; 0/1 for verifier-RL, the reward model's score for RLHF. src§17
Reward hacking
When the RL policy finds adversarial inputs that score highly on the reward model but are actually low quality (e.g., gibberish). src§20
Reward model
A neural network trained on pairwise preferences to predict human preference scores; the reward function in RLHF. src§20
RLHF
Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback: RL where the reward comes from a learned model of human preferences; used for subjective tasks. src§16, §20
Rollout
One sampled trajectory of model outputs in an RL step; multiple rollouts per prompt let RL score and learn from variation. src§17
Sampling
Probabilistically selecting a token from the model's output distribution at each generation step. src§6
Scaling laws
Empirical relationships between training compute, data, parameter count, and performance; the basis for sizing a model. srcgeneral AI literature
Softmax
The function that converts raw logits into a valid probability distribution; the last step before sampling. src§4
Special tokens
Vocabulary tokens added by hand (not learned from data) to mark structural roles: <|im_start|>, role markers, search delimiters. src§3, §10
Supervised fine-tuning (SFT)
The post-training stage where the model is fine-tuned on human-written conversation pairs to behave as an assistant. src§10
System prompt
The hidden message prepended to every conversation that sets the model's identity, behavior, and context. src§12
Temperature
A knob that sharpens or flattens the output distribution at sampling time; low = deterministic, high = creative. src§6
Test-time compute
The compute spent during inference per query, increasingly used as a scaling axis (reasoning models). src§13, §21
Token
The basic unit of input/output — an integer ID corresponding to a subword piece in the vocabulary. src§3
Tool use
The model's ability to emit special tokens that trigger external actions (web search, code execution) with results piped back into context. src§11
Top-k
A sampling restriction limiting choices to the k most probable tokens; common values are 40–50. src§6
Top-p (nucleus sampling)
A sampling restriction limiting choices to the smallest set of top tokens whose probabilities sum to at least p. srcHoltzman et al. 2019
Transformer
The neural network architecture used by all modern LLMs; a stack of identical blocks with attention and MLP sub-layers. srcVaswani et al. 2017
Unembedding
The final layer that maps internal vector representations back into vocabulary-sized logits for the output distribution. src§5
Verifier
An automated checker that scores whether a model output is correct, enabling RL on math, code, and games. src§17
Vocabulary
The fixed set of tokens the model knows; modern frontier models have ~100K–130K tokens in their vocab. src§3
Weight
A single learned parameter in the network; synonymous with "parameter." src§5

Common misconceptions

Twelve beliefs learners commonly hold — with the reality, and the section that addresses each in depth.

✕ "ChatGPT searches the internet for every reply."
Reality

By default the model answers from its parametric memory (training data, frozen at a cutoff). Only when tool use is invoked (web search, browsing) does it actually retrieve fresh information.

Addressed in: §11
✕ "The model knows what year it is."
Reality

It only knows what was in its training cutoff (often a year or more in the past) plus whatever is in the current context — e.g., a system prompt stating today's date.

Addressed in: §7, §11, §12
✕ "Bigger model = always better."
Reality

Bigger means more capable on the hardest queries, but for most tasks a smaller, faster, cheaper model is plenty. Latency, cost, and privacy often matter more than raw capability.

Addressed in: §23
✕ "Hallucinations are a bug that will eventually be patched."
Reality

Hallucinations are a structural property of parametric memory under uncertainty. They can be mitigated (via "I don't know" training and tool use) but not eliminated by patching.

Addressed in: §11
✕ "The model is reasoning like a human when it shows its work."
Reality

Functionally the effect is similar (better answers), but mechanistically it's just spreading computation across more forward passes. There's no introspective "thinking" — there's more compute per query.

Addressed in: §13
✕ "ChatGPT has a stable identity / personality."
Reality

Identity is conditioned by the system prompt and identity-SFT pairs. Change those, and the same underlying weights produce a totally different "personality."

Addressed in: §12
✕ "RLHF makes the model smarter."
Reality

RLHF makes the model more helpful and better aligned with preferences. It does not transcend human-level reasoning the way verifier-based RL can. RLHF is a polish, not a leap.

Addressed in: §20
✕ "DeepSeek-R1 and AlphaGo use the same kind of RL as RLHF."
Reality

R1 and AlphaGo use verifier-based RL (clean reward, can transcend humans). RLHF uses a learned reward model (capped at labeler quality, fragile to overoptimization). Same loop shape, different properties.

Addressed in: §19, §20
✕ "If the model gets 9.9 > 9.11 wrong, it must be dumb overall."
Reality

Jagged intelligence. The same model that fumbles a decimal comparison can solve graduate-level physics. Capability is unevenly distributed; don't extrapolate from one failure.

Addressed in: §15
✕ "Tokenizers are an implementation detail you can ignore."
Reality

Tokenization is the source of half the most famous LLM failures (strawberry Rs, character counting, ROT-13). The model never sees letters; it sees tokens, and that constraint shapes a lot of behavior.

Addressed in: §3, §14
✕ "Context window means memory across conversations."
Reality

The context window is per-session and resets each conversation unless an explicit memory tool is invoked. "ChatGPT remembers me" is either a dedicated memory feature or the history being re-sent every turn.

Addressed in: §4, §10
✕ "Open-weights and open-source mean the same thing."
Reality

"Open-weights" means the trained parameters are published. True open-source would also include the training data, code, and full reproducibility. Most "open" models release only weights.

The Whole Idea in 90 Seconds

If you read only this box

A large language model is trained to do one thing: predict the next word. But doing that well, across all of human writing, is impossible by memory — so the model is forced to compress the text into the principles that generate it: grammar, meaning, facts, reasoning. Those compressed principles are the "intelligence" — and they live as geometry (points in a 4096-dimensional meaning-space) routed through a stack of 80 sculpted lenses (the weights). The same machinery that makes it smart also makes it confidently wrong sometimes, because it only ever optimises for plausible, never true. Everything below is that one story, told in depth.

Stop 1

The Paradox: "It Just Predicts the Next Word"

"Just predicting the next word" is literally true — and it's why people doubt an LLM could be intelligent. The resolution is the spine of this whole course: predicting the next word, at enormous scale, is a forcing function that compels a machine to learn how the world works. Prediction is the exam; understanding is what you must grow to pass it.

You might think

"It's just fancy autocomplete — it can't really be intelligent."

What's really going on

Same objective, vastly harder version. To autocomplete all of human text well, memory isn't enough — you have to learn the rules underneath the words. "Autocomplete" names the interface, not what had to be built behind it.

The Parrot

Cook A — memorises

Has 10,000 recipes word-for-word. Ask for one — flawless. Ask for "a dessert from only what's in my fridge right now" and Cook A freezes: there's no recipe to recall.

→ Reproduces. Helpless with the unseen.
The Mind

Cook B — understands

Studied the same recipes, but stopped memorising and internalised principles: acid cuts fat, heat transforms, salt amplifies. Hand Cook B an anchovy-chocolate-lime fridge and they invent a coherent dish that exists in no cookbook.

→ Generalises. Handles what it never saw.
Analogy · Music

A jazz musician who memorises 10,000 solos can replay them flawlessly — but put them on stage with a rhythm section they've never heard and they freeze. A musician who internalised the principles — chord tensions, rhythmic displacement, tension-and-release — can improvise over any changes. The LLM is trained on so much "music" (text) that rote memorisation can't keep up. It's forced to learn the jazz theory underneath — and that's why it can solo over a chord progression it's never seen.

Analogy · Sports

A cricket batsman who memorises every ball ever bowled can recite pitch maps — but a new bowler with a new action is unreadable. A batsman who internalised the principles — seam position, wrist angle, length cues — reads the delivery in the first 0.2 seconds. The LLM sees so many "deliveries" (contexts) that memorising each one is impossible. To keep scoring runs, it has to learn to read the bowler's hand — the generalisable principles beneath the data.

Analogy · Business

A consultant who memorises 10,000 case studies can quote them verbatim — but a client with a genuinely novel situation gets a blank stare. A consultant who internalised the principles — competitive dynamics, incentive structures, network effects — can diagnose a business they've never seen. The LLM has consumed so many "cases" that recall alone can't predict what comes next. It's pushed to extract the strategic frameworks — and that's why it can advise on situations found in no textbook.

Both cooks saw the same data; the difference is what they compressed it into. An LLM, forced to predict the next word across all of human text, can't be Cook A — too much text, every context slightly new. To keep scoring well it's pushed to become Cook B. Below is the exam itself. Press "Predict next."

Lab · The Next-Token Playground
The entire visible mechanism: read the context, score every possible next word, pick. Watch what it takes to guess well.

To rank "Paris" over "house," the model already has to know things. The simple task hides a deep requirement — and "intelligent" is the name we give to one part of it: taking principles learned in one place and applying them somewhere new (generalisation).

Prediction is the exam. Understanding is what you have to grow in order to keep passing it.

Stop 3 · Anatomy I

From Letters to Meaning-Space

The iceberg told you what the model can do. The next two stops open the engine. Stop 3 is the on-ramp from English to math: what actually happens the moment you press Enter on a prompt? Your letters become numbers, those numbers become positions in a 4096-dimensional map of meaning, and those positions are what flows into everything that follows. This is the sentence the model reads. It is not English.

Step 1 · The tokeniser — a librarian with room numbers

The model can't read letters. It only operates on numbers. So the very first thing that happens is a piece of software called the tokeniser chops your text into pieces and assigns each piece an integer. Imagine a giant librarian who has read essentially the entire public internet and has built a private dictionary of about 100,000 "useful chunks of language" — the vocabulary. Each chunk has a fixed ID, like a hotel room number. Common words are one chunk; rare words get broken into common sub-word pieces. Nothing is ever "out of vocabulary."

You might think

"The model reads words, like we do."

What's really going on

It reads number-coded fragments. This is why it sometimes miscounts the r's in "strawberry" or trips on spelling — the word arrives as the chunks str + aw + berry, not as letters. Its "alphabet" is a vocabulary of 100,000 fragments, not 26 letters.

Analogy · Everyday

Imagine LEGO only sells 100,000 brick shapes. Common objects (a 2×4 brick, a wheel) are single pieces. Rare shapes don't exist as one piece — you snap together standard bricks to approximate them. The tokeniser works the same way: common words are one token; rare words are assembled from common sub-pieces. The model never sees the raw plastic — only pre-moulded bricks with catalogue numbers.

Analogy · Sports

In cricket scoring shorthand, common events have single symbols: · (dot ball), 4 (boundary), W (wicket). Unusual events are described by combining standard symbols. The tokeniser does the same: "the" is one token (common event = one symbol), but "epistemologically" has no single symbol — it's assembled from standard pieces like a complex scoring sequence. The scorer's shorthand, not the cricket itself.

Step 1.5 · How the chunking actually happens — the "glue-rule" trick (BPE)

Now the part most explanations skip: how does the librarian decide where to chop? She doesn't try lots of combinations and pick the best one. She uses a much simpler trick called Byte Pair Encoding (BPE) that runs in two phases — one that happens once, and one that happens every time you press Enter.

Phase 1 · happens ONCE, before you ever type

The vocabulary is built

Start with every character as a token (~256 of them). Scan the entire internet. Find the most common adjacent pair. Glue them into a new token. Write down the rule. Repeat ~100,000 times. The vocab + the ordered glue-rules are then frozen and shipped with the model.

rule #1t · h → th rule #2th · e → the rule #14i · n → in rule #87t · o → to rule #243to · k → tok rule #1102i · z → iz rule #4521tok · en → token rule #9817iz · ation → ization …~100,000 rules in total
Phase 2 · happens every time you type

Your text is tokenised by replay

Start at the bottom: break your word into single characters. Walk down the rule list from rule #1 to rule #100,000. For each rule, ask: "do I see these two tokens adjacent anywhere?" If yes, glue. If no, skip. No searching. No backtracking. Deterministic: same input → always the same chunks. Watch the librarian tokenise tokenization below.

This is why "strawberry" miscounts r's — it arrived as str + aw + berry, three opaque chunks with the letter-count buried inside each.

You might think

The tokeniser is intelligent — it decides how to chunk your word based on what makes sense.

What's really going on

It's a mechanical replay of merges learned once on the internet, applied in fixed order. The tokeniser has no idea token is a meaningful English word. It just knows that during training, tok and en happened to be common adjacent neighbours, so rule #4521 says "glue them." The "intelligence" is entirely upstream — it lives in the corpus that taught which pairs were common.

Analogy · Business

A company starts with full phrases: "as soon as possible," "return on investment," "end of day." Over time, the most frequent phrases get shortened to acronyms: ASAP, ROI, EOD. Nobody decided these were "meaningful units" — they just appeared together so often that compressing them saved everyone time. BPE does exactly this: it counts which character pairs appear together most often in the training corpus and glues them into a single token. The resulting "acronyms" are the vocabulary.

Analogy · Everyday

WhatsApp groups evolve their own shorthand. "Good morning" becomes GM. "On my way" becomes OMW. "Talk to you later" becomes TTYL. Nobody published a dictionary — the abbreviations emerged from frequency. BPE is the same process run at industrial scale: scan trillions of characters, find the pairs that appear together most, glue them, repeat 100,000 times. The resulting vocabulary is a chat group's shorthand — optimised for the internet's most common patterns.

Step 2 · The lookup — each ID becomes a vector

The integer ID is just an address. The model now goes to a giant lookup table — the embedding matrix — and pulls out the long list of numbers stored at that address. That list, typically 4,096 numbers long, becomes the model's actual internal representation of the token. The IDs are pointers; the vectors are the meaning.

Step 3 · What does the vector mean? — the Spotify analogy

Why 4,096 numbers? Why those numbers? Two analogies make this real.

Spotify doesn't store you as the name "Ram." It stores you as a vector of ≈200 numbers — your affinity to each of 200 hidden dimensions of taste the algorithm discovered. Nobody at Spotify labelled the dimensions. They emerged because grouping listeners that way was useful for prediction. Word embeddings work exactly the same way
Analogy · Music

Music-recognition apps like Shazam don't store songs as audio files — they store each song as a vector of acoustic features: tempo, key, spectral centroid, onset density, harmonic complexity. Two songs that "sound similar" have vectors that point in the same direction. A ballad and a death-metal track point in opposite directions. Word embeddings work identically: each word is stored as a vector of linguistic features the model discovered, and "similar meaning" means "nearby vectors."

Analogy · Medicine

A doctor doesn't store a patient as a name — they store them as a vector of symptoms and vitals: temperature, blood pressure, white cell count, pain score, oxygen saturation. Two patients with similar vectors likely have the same condition — even if they described their complaints in completely different words. Word embeddings do the same: each word gets a "symptom profile" across 4,096 hidden dimensions. Words with similar profiles cluster together — "cat" and "kitten" are in the same diagnostic neighbourhood; "treaty" is in a different ward entirely.

Each of the 4,096 numbers attached to cat is the word's score on some hidden dimension of meaning the model discovered. Some dimension turned out to track "is-an-animal." Another, "is-household." Another, "appears-near-yarn-or-laps-or-purring." Nobody told the model these dimensions exist. They emerged because grouping words that way kept prediction error low. (This principle has a real name in linguistics — the distributional hypothesis: "you shall know a word by the company it keeps." See Stop 9.)

Step 4.5 · Position-stamps — how word order survives

One quiet detail before we move on. The vectors so far know what a token means, but not where it sat in the sentence — and word order obviously matters ("dog bites man" ≠ "man bites dog"). So alongside each meaning-vector, the model adds a small position vector — a position-stamp that says "I'm word #3" or "I'm word #47." Same vector size, just added on top. Now each input vector carries both what I am and where I sit. Without this trick, attention (next stop) would be permutation-blind — it could see all the words but not their order. The position-stamp is small; you don't need to see it. You just need to know it's there.

Step 4 · Why "nearness" means similarity — the city-map analogy

Tokyo is at (35.7° N, 139.7° E). Osaka is at (34.7° N, 135.5° E). Two numbers each — and you can immediately ask "how close are they?" Tokyo and Buenos Aires (−34.6°, −58.4°) are far. You used just two coordinates and could meaningfully compute "how near."

Word embeddings do the same — but in 4,096 dimensions instead of 2. You can't visualise a 4,096-D map, but the math of "how close are two points?" is unchanged. Words used in similar contexts ended up with similar vectors — pulled toward each other by billions of training nudges. cat sits near dog, near kitten, near pet. treaty is on the other side of the galaxy.

Step 5 · The keystone — meaning has become geometry

Now the famous demonstration. Take the vector for king. Subtract man. Add woman. You get a point in space that is almost exactly the vector for queen. The space, after training, has a consistent "royalty direction" and a consistent "gender direction" — and you can do arithmetic on meaning. Click the demo below to watch the arrows.

Meaning has become geometry. The model doesn't store "queen" — it stores a direction from "king" that matches the direction from "man" to "woman."
You might think

The embedding for "cat" contains what cat means — like a tiny dictionary entry.

What's really going on

The embedding for "cat" is meaningless on its own. It's only meaningful in relation to every other embedding. Cat-ness is "the place you end up when you move from dog-ness in the small-pet direction." Meaning is positional — it's where you are in the space relative to everything else.

The output of this stop: your sentence is now a stack of vectors, one per token, each a point in the 4,096-D meaning-space. The next stop shows what 80 sculpted layers do to that stack to focus it onto an answer.

Stop 4 · Anatomy II

Attention & the Stack of Lenses

Stop 3 gave you the input: a stack of points in meaning-space. This stop is what those points flow through. Modern LLMs are built on one architectural trick — discovered in 2017, in a paper called "Attention Is All You Need" — that broke the previous generation of language models open. We'll start with a puzzle that shows why this trick exists, then open the engine.

The puzzle that attention solves

Read these two sentences:

The trophy didn't fit in the suitcase because it was too big.
The trophy didn't fit in the suitcase because it was too small.

Same sentence, one word different. But "it" flips referent — trophy in the first, suitcase in the second. Your brain does that without breaking stride. How does a machine? The previous generation of language models (RNNs and LSTMs) processed text on a conveyor belt — one word at a time, keeping a running summary in memory. By word twenty, the trophy had blurred into noise. The Transformer threw the conveyor belt away. Toggle below — watch how "it" rewires.

"it" attends most strongly to → trophy

Same sentence, one word changed at the far right — and "it" rewires what it points to. This is impossible without every word being able to see every other word. That ability is attention.

How "it" decides — the dating-app analogy (Q, K, V)

Every word emits three things. A Query — "here's what I'm looking for" ("it" emits something like: I'm a pronoun, I need a recent noun, probably the subject of the clause). A Key — "here's what I am" ("trophy" emits: I'm a noun, I'm the subject, I'm physical, I have size). A Value — "here's the content I'd contribute." The model takes "it"'s Query, compares it against every other word's Key, and pulls in their Values weighted by how strong the match is. "trophy" scores high. "the" scores near zero. "suitcase" scores moderately. That weighted blend becomes "it"'s new representation — the version of "it" that now knows what it refers to.

Analogy · Business

A CEO walks into a boardroom with a question (Query): "Who here knows about our supply-chain risk?" Each person holds up a name badge (Key) describing their expertise: "logistics," "legal," "finance." The CEO compares the question against every badge simultaneously and pays most attention to logistics — but still listens a bit to finance and legal. Each person then shares their briefing (Value), weighted by how relevant their badge was. The CEO's updated understanding is a weighted blend of everyone's input — not a single answer from one person.

Analogy · Sports

A cricket captain setting the field asks a question (Query): "Where will this batsman play the next ball?" Every fielder holds a card (Key) with what they've noticed: "he's been driving through covers," "he steps down to spinners," "he edges bouncers." The captain compares the question against every card, weights the cover fielder highest (strongest signal), and combines their positioning suggestions (Values) into one field placement. The result isn't one fielder's opinion — it's a blend weighted by relevance.

You might think

Attention works like human attention — the model is "focusing" on one important word, like a spotlight narrowing in.

What's really going on

It's not narrowing. Every position attends to every other position — just with different weights. It's a weighted vote, not a spotlight. Nothing is excluded; some voices are just louder. That's why "it" can reach back twenty words to "trophy" with no degradation — there's no chain of intermediaries to muddy the signal.

"Every position attends to every other position" is powerful — but it has a cost. If your prompt is n tokens long, attention computes n × n comparisons. Double the prompt length and you quadruple the work: O(n²).

This is why models have a context window — a maximum number of tokens they can process at once. Early GPT models had 2,048 tokens (~1,500 words). GPT-4 pushed to 128K. Claude can handle 200K. But the cost is always quadratic: a 200K-token context costs 10,000× more attention computation than a 200-token one.

Researchers are actively working on sub-quadratic attention — methods like sparse attention, linear attention, and sliding-window approaches that approximate the full n×n matrix without computing every cell. These trade a small accuracy loss for dramatically lower cost at long contexts.

The practical takeaway: context window = the model's working memory. Everything outside it simply doesn't exist for that forward pass. This is why "memory" features save notes externally and paste them back in — they're working around a hard architectural limit.

Multi-head — not one panel of experts, several

One round of Q/K/V is powerful but narrow — it can only ask one kind of question at a time. So the Transformer does this in parallel, 32 or 64 times, with different learned Q/K/V projections each time. We call them heads. One head specialises in pronoun antecedents. Another tracks subject-verb agreement. Another tracks tone. Another tracks code indentation. Nobody told them what to specialise in — they drifted into roles during training, the way different cooks in a busy kitchen claim their stations.

Head 3 · pronoun → antecedent
Head 7 · subject ↔ verb agreement
Head 14 · broad topic context
Head 22 · the word right before

Each head, a different pattern. Each layer has ~32 of these firing in parallel. Across 80 layers, that's thousands of specialised "panels of experts" voting on every token.

One head is a perspective. Many heads in parallel, across many layers, is how the model thinks from many angles at once.

Attention is only half of a layer — the MLP is the other half

One honest correction before we stack. Each Transformer "layer" actually has two halves, applied in sequence: attention (the mechanism we just opened) decides who-talks-to-whom — which other tokens each position should pull information from. Then a feed-forward MLP (multi-layer perceptron) decides what each token becomes next — a dense local transformation applied position-by-position. Attention routes information across positions; the MLP transforms it within a position. The canal-network metaphor below fits the MLP especially well — it's where most of the model's parameters actually live, and where most of the "world facts" are widely believed to be stored.

Then it stacks — 80 lenses, depth recapitulating the iceberg

A Transformer isn't one round of attention — it's ~80 of them stacked, each layer's output feeding the next. And here's the beautiful part: the lower layers attend over fairly literal stuff ("is the word two positions to my left a noun?"). The middle layers attend over phrases ("what's the verb of the clause I'm in?"). The upper layers attend over deeply abstract patterns ("who's the betrayed party in this narrative?"). The depth of the stack is the depth of the iceberg.

Each lens bends the signal a specific way. The full stack focuses the prompt onto a single output point — the next token. Mess up a few lens curvatures and the beam scatters into gibberish. Get them all right, and the beam comes out sharp.

Stacking 80 layers creates a problem: by the time the signal reaches layer 60, it's been bent so many times that the original input might be unrecognisable. Early deep networks suffered from this — the vanishing gradient problem — where information simply evaporated through too many transformations.

The fix is elegant: residual connections (also called "skip connections"). After each layer transforms the input, the original input is added back to the output. So instead of output = transform(input), each layer computes output = input + transform(input).

Think of it as a highway bypass: the main road goes through the city (the transformation), but there's also a highway that carries the original signal straight past. The layer only needs to learn what to add — the refinement — not reconstruct everything from scratch. This is why 80 layers can stack without the signal dying: the original information has a direct path through the entire network.

Without residual connections, training a Transformer would be essentially impossible. They're invisible in the architecture diagrams but load-bearing in practice.

A second metaphor — weights as a canal network

The lens-stack helps you feel depth. The canal-network helps you feel routing. Picture a vast network of canals carved into a landscape. Water (your input vectors) flows in from one side and comes out the other. Some channels are wide — water flows through fast and largely intact (high positive weight). Some are narrow — water trickles through, the flow gets damped (low weight). Some subtract from the next channel they meet (negative weight). The water splits, merges, recombines. By the time it reaches the far end, it has concentrated into a specific output — and the shape of the canal network is the model. The weights ARE the channel widths and connections. They were carved during training. That's what a billion weights does, in physical terms.

One layer of the canal network. Channel widths = weight strengths. Water (vectors) flows in on the left, splits across the network, concentrates on the right. Now imagine 79 more of these walls stacked behind it — that's the full model. The intelligence isn't in any channel; it's in the pattern of widths, across the whole stack. Stop 5 zooms into this exact mechanism and shows how it gets carved.

You might think

Somewhere inside the model there's a weight (or a small group of weights) that stores the fact "cats are pets" — like a row in a hidden lookup table.

What's really going on

There is no such weight. The fact "cats are pets" is distributed across millions of weights, none of which individually contains it. It's an emergent property of how millions of weights collectively bend the canal water / focus the lens beam. Delete one weight: nothing happens. Delete a thousand: slight wobble. There is no "cat fact" channel to delete. This is called distributed representation, and it's the deepest weird thing about neural networks.

Attention, stacked, is how the iceberg gets deep.

No single weight knows — distributed representation, demonstrated

One last beat before the closing summary. The deepest weird thing about neural networks: no single weight "knows" anything. The fact "cats are pets" is smeared across millions of weights, none of which individually contains it. Pretend you have a way to delete weights. Try the buttons:

Pick an experiment. Then ask: where did the fact "cats are pets" actually live?

There is no row in the weights file labelled "cats are pets." If you printed out every weight in the model and read them all, you would not find that fact anywhere. You would just find numbers. And yet, when the model runs, the fact reliably emerges. Distributed representation — and the reason we can't easily edit a model's beliefs. There's no cell to edit. The belief lives in the collective geometry.

The full forward pass — what every single layer actually does

Here's the slightly-more-detailed version, in plain language. Every step is the same three operations — multiply by weights, add weights, squash — repeated. No "if-then" logic. No rules. No database lookup. Just multiplication and addition with weights, end to end.

1
Your text becomes a stack of vectors

6 tokens → 6 IDs → 6 lookups in the embedding matrix → a 6 × 4,096 grid of numbers sitting at the doorway of the Transformer. This is the "water."

2
Layer 1 transforms it

The 6 × 4,096 grid gets multiplied by a giant weight matrix (say 4,096 × 4,096), then a bias is added, then it passes through a non-linear "squash" function. Out pops a new 6 × 4,096 grid — your sentence, bent. output = squash(W · input + b)

3
Layer 2 transforms what Layer 1 produced

Same operation. Different weights. Each layer has its own weight matrices. The output of one layer is the input of the next.

4
Repeat for 80 layers

Attention + MLP at every layer. By layer 30 the lower layers' "syntax patterns" have been transformed into mid-layer "meaning patterns." By layer 70 those have become upper-layer "narrative patterns." The same input, bent ~80 times by ~80 different weight walls.

5
The final layer's output is mapped back to vocabulary

The 4,096-D vector at the "next-token slot" gets multiplied by the embedding matrix transposed. Out comes a score — a logit — for every word in the 100,000-token vocabulary.

6
Softmax → probabilities → sample

The 100,000 logits get squashed by softmax into a probability distribution. A sampler (set by temperature — see Stop 6) picks one token. That's the next word you see.

Every step: multiply by weights, add weights, squash, repeat. The intelligence — the fact that "The cat sat on the" produces "mat" rather than "spaghetti" — is entirely a function of which exact numbers fill those weight matrices. Different numbers → different output. Same operation; different model.

Embeddings are what flows. Weights are the shape of the riverbed. Intelligence lives in how the riverbed has been carved.
The forward pass, end-to-end

Now you can read the whole sentence the model reads. When you press Enter on a prompt: 1) the tokeniser shreds your text into integer IDs · 2) each ID retrieves a 4,096-number vector from the embedding matrix (Stop 3) · 3) that stack of vectors enters Layer 1 — a wall of attention heads + an MLP — and gets bent · 4) the output is fed into Layer 2 · 5) repeat 80 times, each layer's canal network adding its bend · 6) the final vector at the "next token" position gets multiplied by the embedding matrix transposed, producing a score for every word in the vocabulary · 7) a softmax turns scores into probabilities · 8) a sampler picks one token. That's it. Every capability in the iceberg — every behaviour you experience — happens inside that pipeline.

Stop 5 is about how the lenses got curved and how the canals got widened in the first place. Stop 6 is what happens when you press Enter on the same prompt twice. After that, we measure how smart all of this actually is, and where it breaks.

How It Forms — Sculpting the Network

Stops 3 and 4 showed you the anatomy: the embedding geometry and the canal network of attention layers. This stop shows you how that anatomy got the way it is. Nobody hand-programmed the channel widths. They emerged from one relentless pressure — and they live in the model's weights. The wall-of-valves animation below is what one layer of the canal network looks like up close. There are 80 of these stacked.

1

Ingest an ocean of text

Trillions of words — books, code, conversations, science. Far more than any system could store verbatim.

2

One objective: predict the next token

Hide the next token, guess it, measure the error, nudge every weight in the network a hair. Repeat at unimaginable scale.

3

Memorising becomes impossible

Too much text, every context a little new. A lookup table can't keep up.

4

So it is forced to compress

The only way to keep predicting well is to discover the rules that generate the text — and store those instead. Compression is the birth of understanding.

5

The rules crystallise as geometry + routing

Embedding distances pull similar words together (Stop 3). Canal widths route inputs to outputs (Stop 4). Each emerges because it lowers prediction error.

An ocean of words can't be stored — so it's squeezed into the shape of the canal network and the positions of words in meaning-space. That squeeze is the learning.

Prediction is the pressure. Understanding-shaped structure is the only thing that relieves it.
Analogy · Everyday

In a darkroom, a photograph emerges gradually: the paper starts blank, the chemical bath slowly reveals shapes, details sharpen, and the developer decides when to stop. Training works the same way. The weights start random (blank paper). Billions of examples flow through (the chemical bath). Structure gradually emerges — first gross patterns, then fine details. The trainer decides when to "fix" the image (freeze the weights). Over-develop and you get noise; under-develop and the picture is blurry. The final photograph is the model.

Analogy · Everyday

A learner driver starts with random instincts — jerky steering, late braking, no lane discipline. Each driving lesson is a training example: the instructor says "you braked too late" (the error signal), and the student adjusts. After thousands of hours, the student doesn't think about steering — the principles have been compressed into muscle memory. The weights are that muscle memory: billions of tiny adjustments, none of which individually "knows" how to drive, but whose pattern produces smooth driving on roads the student has never seen.

A weight, up close — the wall of valves

Zoom into one layer of the canal network and look at one junction. A "weight" is just one valve's setting — a single number, like 0.0273 or −0.4912 — that decides how much signal passes through that junction and which way it leans. "Parameters" is the count of all the valves across all 80 layers. Below: one layer's worth, animated.

Running the model — one layer of the stack. Pour a token's vector in on the left; it cascades through the valves; a transformed vector pools out on the right. A single valve is meaningless — the pattern of all of them, across all 80 layers, decides whether the output focuses on "Paris" or scatters into noise. Now imagine 79 more of these walls behind it.

How do the valves get set? Training.

They start random — water in, gibberish out. Training pours billions of example sentences through, checks how wrong the bottom came out, and nudges every valve a hair toward "less wrong." A trillion nudges later, the wall has settled into one very particular configuration. The same nudging pressure also pulls embedding vectors together: every time "cat" and "dog" appeared in similar contexts, their points in meaning-space drifted closer.

Training. Example text flows through, the guess is checked against the real next word, and every valve is tuned a hair (the rule for which way = gradients / backpropagation). The glowing valves are "adjusting." When it stops, you freeze them all.

You might think

"Weights are knobs that control how the network is trained."

What's really going on

Weights aren't the control panel for training — they're the output of it. Training is the sculptor; the weights are the statue. The pattern of all billion settings is the knowledge itself. One weight means nothing; the configuration is the magic sauce.

A single synapse strength in your brain is meaningless, but the pattern of trillions is your memory. Weights are artificial synapses. Or: one fader on a city-sized mixing board does nothing; the full configuration is the song. Two ways to feel the same fact
Analogy · Music

A recording studio's mixing console has thousands of faders — one per channel, per frequency band, per effect send. No single fader "is" the song. But the exact position of every fader — the configuration — defines precisely how the raw tracks become the final mix. Move one fader: imperceptible. Randomise all of them: noise. The weights are the fader positions. The song is what emerges when signal flows through that specific configuration.

Analogy · Everyday

A city's road network has thousands of intersections, each with its own lane widths, speed limits, and signal timings. No single intersection "knows" how to get from the airport to the stadium. But the pattern of all the road widths and connections determines how traffic flows — and reroutes smoothly when one road is blocked. The weights are lane widths and signal timings. The intelligence is the traffic flow that emerges from the whole network, not from any single junction.

Modern LLMs aren't trained in one shot. They go through three distinct phases, each with a different objective:

Pre-training
Phase 1 · months · massive compute

Pure next-token prediction on trillions of words from the internet, books, and code. This is where ~95% of the model's knowledge and capabilities form. No human labels — the text supervises itself. The weights go from random to principled.

Supervised Fine-Tuning (SFT)
Phase 2 · days · human examples

Human annotators write thousands of ideal (prompt, response) pairs. The model is fine-tuned on these examples to learn the format of being helpful: answer questions directly, follow instructions, refuse harmful requests. Knowledge from Phase 1; manners from Phase 2.

RLHF / RLAIF
Phase 3 · days · preference ranking

The model generates multiple responses. Humans (or another AI) rank them: "Response A is better than Response B." A reward model learns what "better" means, and the base model is nudged toward higher-reward outputs. This phase polishes tone, reduces harmful outputs, and aligns behaviour with human preferences.

The key insight: Phase 1 builds the engine; Phases 2–3 install the steering wheel. A pre-trained-only model is a brilliant but antisocial savant — it can complete any text pattern but won't helpfully answer questions. SFT and RLHF teach it to be a useful conversationalist without fundamentally changing what it knows.

Where the intelligence actually lives

Freeze every valve, write the billions of settings to a list, save it — that list is the gigabytes file. "Weights" are nothing more exotic than the frozen channel widths.

So the intelligence isn't in any valve, and it isn't in the water — it's in the pattern, visible only when something flows through. Copy that file to a blank machine and it's just as smart, because you copied the configuration. And the numbers don't store answers like a dictionary — they store a way of routing any input to a sensible output, which is exactly why it can answer things it never saw.

Stop 6

How It Runs — One Word at a Time

The sauce is frozen after training. But running it raises two puzzles people trip on: why one token at a time? and why isn't the output the same every time? Both have clean answers — and now you have the anatomy to see why.

Why one token at a time?

The only skill in the sauce is "predict the next token, given everything so far." A full answer is that one skill run in a loop — each new token fed back in as input for the next. This is called autoregressive generation, and it mirrors exactly how the model was trained.

Lab · Autoregression in motion
Each predicted token becomes part of the input for the next prediction. Watch it build, then loop. (Shown as whole words for legibility; real tokens are word-pieces — see Stop 3.)
↑ each committed token is fed back in — so token 6 is shaped by the actual tokens 1–5

Why not emit the whole answer at once? Three reasons.

Reason 1 · Coherence

Word 5 needs word 4

Each word constrains the next. Emit them independently and they wouldn't agree. Committing one and feeding it back keeps everything consistent.

Reason 2 · The maths

Chain-rule factorisation

P(whole text) is impossibly complex to model directly. Factoring it into P(w₁)·P(w₂|w₁)·P(w₃|w₁,w₂)… turns one impossible problem into many tractable next-token ones.

Reason 3 · It does parallelise

…but only within a step

One forward pass — all 80 layers running once — reads the whole context at once (that's what attention enables). What can't be parallelised is generating new tokens, because each depends on the previous.

The skill is "one token, well." Everything else is that skill, run in a loop.

Why isn't the output identical every time?

Surprising answer: the model itself is deterministic. The frozen weights don't output an answer — they output a probability distribution over the next token, and that landscape is the same every time for the same prompt. The randomness lives in a separate step — decoding — where we pick a token.

You might think

"If the weights are fixed, surely the recipe gives the same dish every time."

What's really going on

It does — at temperature 0 (always take the top token), output basically repeats. We usually sample instead (a weighted die, set by temperature) because it reads better. Same fixed landscape; different roll of the dice.

Analogy · Everyday

Imagine a restaurant where the waiter always recommends the most popular dish (temperature 0 — "the chicken, every time"). At temperature 0.7, the waiter says "the chicken is most popular, but tonight's fish special is great too" — usually the safe pick, occasionally a pleasant surprise. At temperature 1.2, the waiter enthusiastically suggests the experimental off-menu fusion dish. Same kitchen, same menu, same quality of food — only the adventurousness of the recommendation changes.

Analogy · Everyday

Think of a radio tuning dial. At temperature 0, you're locked on the strongest station — clear signal, no surprises, always the same channel. At 0.7, you're tuned close but allow slight drift — mostly the main station, occasionally catching an interesting neighbouring frequency. At 1.2, you're scanning freely — you'll discover amazing stations but also land on static. The broadcast tower (the model's probability distribution) never changes; only how tightly you lock the dial determines what you hear.

One of the most surprising discoveries: you can teach a frozen model a new task just by showing it examples in the prompt. Give it three examples of "translate English to French" and it translates a fourth — despite never being explicitly trained on translation instructions.

This is called in-context learning (ICL), and it has three flavours:

Zero-shot: "Translate 'hello' to French." No examples — the model infers the task from the instruction alone.
Few-shot: "hello → bonjour, goodbye → au revoir, thank you → ?" The examples define the pattern; the model extends it.
Many-shot: Dozens of examples, pushing the pattern deeper into the context window.

How does this work if no weights change? The leading theory: during pre-training, the model saw millions of "pattern → continuation" sequences. ICL activates induction heads (specialised attention circuits) that recognise "I've seen this pattern before in the context" and apply it. The weights already contain the ability to follow patterns; the prompt just specifies which pattern.

The limit: ICL vanishes when the context window ends. It's powerful but temporary — a working-memory trick, not real learning.

You have two ways to steer a model: change the prompt (the input) or change the weights (the model itself). Here's when each makes sense:

Prompting (including ICL, system prompts, and few-shot examples) is fast, reversible, and requires no training infrastructure. It works well for most tasks: style changes, format control, domain-specific instructions. The model stays general; the prompt does the specialising.

Fine-tuning — running additional training on your own data — permanently modifies a copy of the weights. It's expensive, slow, and requires curation. But it wins when you need the model to deeply absorb a domain (medical terminology, legal reasoning, your company's codebase) or reliably follow a format that prompting can't enforce.

The mental model: prompting is coaching from the sideline (quick adjustments, same player). Fine-tuning is months of specialised training (different player at the end, better at one sport, potentially worse at others). Most users will never need to fine-tune — prompting is that powerful. But when you do, you're sculpting new canal-network patterns into the weights themselves.

Stop 7

How Smart Is It, Really?

If intelligence is compressed principles, a fair question is: does a bigger model = a smarter one, and is there a number for "how intelligent"? Short answers: not linearly, and not exactly.

You might think

"A 10-billion-parameter model is 10× smarter than a 1-billion one."

What's really going on

More parameters = more capacity, with strongly diminishing returns — nowhere near linear. And size is only one of three levers; across generations, param count barely predicts capability at all.

Lever 1

Parameters

How many valves the canal network has — how many principles it can hold. Bigger helps — sub-linearly. (Cook B's brain size.)

Lever 2

Data (amount & quality)

The Chinchilla finding: many big models were under-trained. A smaller model fed more, cleaner data beats a bigger one fed less. (Years at the stove.)

Lever 3

Compute + post-training

How long you train, plus RLHF / reasoning tuning. A well-tuned small model can crush an older giant.

Is there a "% intelligence" score?

No single agreed number — but a basket of imperfect yardsticks. Each measures a slice; none captures "intelligence" whole, and all are vulnerable to contamination and saturation.

Benchmarks

Capability tests

MMLU (broad knowledge), GSM8K / MATH (maths), HumanEval (coding), GPQA (hard science). Models publish scores on each.

Leaderboards

Human preference

Chatbot Arena (LMArena) ranks models by an Elo rating from millions of head-to-head votes — the closest thing to one overall number, but it measures preference, not intelligence.

Generalisation

Novel reasoning

ARC-AGI deliberately tests fluid reasoning on unseen puzzles rather than memorised facts — an attempt to measure intelligence-as-generalisation.

So what does "intelligent" even mean here?

Back to the spine: intelligence ≈ taking principles learned in one place and applying them to a situation never seen before. Operationally, a blend of generalisation, breadth, reasoning depth, and learning from few examples.

But there's no settled definition even for humans — so for models it's part measurable competence, part projection (the seafloor of the iceberg).

"Intelligent" is a direction, not a dial.

A spectrum, not a switch

Not everything a model does is equally "intelligent." Some outputs are near-pure recall (parrot end); some are real generalisation (mind end). Click an example to see what's happening underneath.

Stop 8

Where It Breaks — and How We Patch It

The same machinery that makes the model smart also makes it confidently wrong. Understanding why is the most useful thing on this page — and it ends on a hopeful twist from the world of code.

You might think

"Hallucination is the model coughing up random, irrelevant words because it's unsure."

What's really going on

Almost the opposite. It's fluent and confident — and still wrong. Random word-salad would mean it's unsure (a flat distribution). Hallucination is usually a sharply confident guess that happens to be false, because "confident" only ever meant "plausible," never "true."

The one sharp idea: the principles it learned are principles of plausibility, not truth — and there's no separate faculty that checks the result against reality (Cook B never tasted). Two parallel "canals" — but only one is connected.

Two tracks, only one wired. Whatever question you ask, the plausibility engine fires fluently end-to-end. The truth-check track was never built. The model has no internal alarm for "I don't know" — because there is no sensor that could trigger it. Pick a question type below and watch the same plausibility-fires/truth-silent pattern in four flavours.

The plausibility engine has no idea what truth is. That's the bug — and the brilliance.
Plausibility engine
Truth check
Analogy · Medicine

Imagine a doctor who has read every medical textbook ever written but has never examined a patient. When you describe symptoms, they confidently diagnose based on pattern-matching across all their reading — and they're right most of the time, because textbook patterns are reliable. But for a rare combination of symptoms, they'll confidently name a plausible-sounding condition that doesn't quite fit — because they have no way to check their diagnosis against reality. They can't run a blood test; they can only say "this sounds like..." Hallucination is the model being this doctor: fluent, well-read, confident — and occasionally wrong in ways that sound perfectly right.

Analogy · Sports

A cricket commentator working from audio only (no video feed) narrates the match based on crowd noise, bat sounds, and scorer's signals. Most of the time they're spot-on — a loud crack plus roaring crowd means a boundary. But occasionally they confidently call "that's gone for six!" when it was actually caught at the rope. They can't see the field — they can only infer from the signals they receive. The model similarly has no "video feed" to reality. It narrates from patterns in text (its only signal), and sometimes calls a six when the ball was caught.

Limit 1

The grounding gap

It learned from text about the world, never the world. Its grasp of fire or gravity is the shape those words make in language — like knowing a city only from its subway map, never the street.

Limit 2

No separate referee

The faculty that writes the answer is the only one judging it, and it can only ask "is this plausible?" — never "is this true?" No reliable alarm for "I'm past what I know," so it extrapolates with even confidence everywhere.

The hopeful twist: code has a "tongue"

Why does an AI feel almost magic at coding? Because code is special — it has the one thing the raw model lacks: a free, instant referee.

A

What it was fed

Not just code — code paired with intent: docstrings, comments, Stack Overflow ("I want X → broken code → fix → why"), commit diffs, and tests sitting beside implementations.

B

The principles it compressed

Syntax as physics; names have lives (defined before use); an execution model (what a loop does); idioms (intent→implementation recipes); decomposition. The cooking laws, for code.

C

What emerged

Completion, generation from plain English, translation between languages, explanation, debugging, refactoring, test-writing — and composing a program for a spec that exists nowhere.

D

The tongue

The raw model still writes plausible code (and will confidently call a method that doesn't exist — a hallucinated API). But you can just run it: the interpreter and tests are ground truth. A coding agent that loops generate → run → read error → fix bolts the missing truth-check onto the plausibility engine. For code, we found Cook B a tongue.

This is the general cure, too: pair the model with something that can verify (tools, search, calculators, tests) and you recover the grounding it never had.

The model's knowledge is frozen at training time — it can't know about yesterday's news or your company's internal docs. RAG bolts on a live knowledge source:

User question → Search relevant docs → Retrieved passages → LLM generates answer

Instead of asking the model to answer from memory, you first retrieve relevant documents (from a database, the web, or internal files), paste them into the prompt, and ask the model to answer based on those sources. The model still does what it's good at — reasoning over text — but now the text is fresh and grounded.

Why it helps with hallucination: the model can cite the retrieved passage. If the answer isn't in the sources, a well-prompted RAG system says "I don't have information on that" rather than fabricating. It's not perfect — the model can still misinterpret the passage — but it turns "generate from frozen memory" into "read and synthesise from live evidence."

The tradeoff: RAG systems are slower (retrieval adds latency), require infrastructure (a vector database, an indexing pipeline), and are only as good as their retrieval — if the search misses the right document, the model still has to guess.

The "code has a tongue" insight generalises: if you give the model access to tools — a calculator, a web search engine, a code interpreter, a database — it can verify its own claims, fetch live data, and take actions in the world. An agent is a model in a loop:

Think → Choose a tool → Read the result → Decide next step → Repeat or answer

The model isn't just generating text — it's planning and executing. A coding agent writes code, runs it, reads the error, fixes it, runs again. A research agent searches the web, reads pages, synthesises findings, and searches again if the answer is incomplete.

Why this matters: tools are the "tongue" from the code example, generalised. The model's plausibility engine stays the same — but now it's wired to external truth-checkers. The grounding gap from the two-track diagram above gets partially bridged: not by changing the model, but by giving it sensors.

The limit: the model is still the decision-maker. It chooses which tool to call and how to interpret the result. A confident-but-wrong model can misuse tools just as confidently — calling the wrong API, misreading a search result, or ignoring contradictory evidence. Tools reduce hallucination; they don't eliminate the underlying architecture that produces it.

Checkpoint You should now understand why hallucination happens (plausibility without truth-checking), why it's architectural not accidental, and how tools/RAG/agents partially bridge the grounding gap.

These image-vectors enter the same Transformer stack alongside text-vectors. The attention mechanism doesn't care whether a vector came from a word or a pixel patch — it just computes Q/K/V matches. This is why modern models can "see" a photo and discuss it: the image is converted into the same meaning-space geometry that text occupies.
Standard Transformers send every token through every parameter. MoE models have many parallel "expert" sub-networks in each layer, but a router sends each token to only 2–4 experts. Result: a model can have hundreds of billions of total parameters but only activate a fraction per token — dramatically faster at inference. The canal-network analogy: instead of one wide canal, you have many specialised channels, and each drop of water is routed to the channels most relevant to it. Trade-off: harder to train, harder to balance expert utilisation.
Partially, not provably. RLHF and constitutional AI train models to refuse harmful requests, but these are behavioural guardrails — learned preferences, not hard constraints. They can be bypassed by clever prompting ("jailbreaks") because the refusal is a soft pattern in the weights, not a firewall. The deeper challenge — alignment — asks: can we ensure that as models become more capable, they pursue goals we actually want? This is an open research problem, not a solved engineering one. Current models are "aligned enough" for current capabilities; whether that scales is genuinely unknown.
Stop 3 mentioned "position-stamps" briefly — here's the detail. Attention is order-blind by design: it compares every token to every other, regardless of position. To restore order, a positional encoding is added to each embedding before it enters the stack. Early Transformers used fixed sine-wave patterns; modern models use RoPE (Rotary Position Embeddings), which encode relative distance by rotating the Q and K vectors. The result: attention naturally weighs nearby tokens slightly more, and the model can tell "dog bites man" from "man bites dog."
A curated slice of the internet + licensed sources. Common Crawl (a public web archive) forms the base — but raw web text is noisy, so it goes through aggressive filtering: deduplication, language detection, quality scoring, removal of toxic or personally identifiable content. On top of this, labs add books, academic papers, code repositories (GitHub), and increasingly synthetic data (text generated by other models, then filtered for quality). The mix and curation matter enormously — "garbage in, garbage out" is real. The specifics of each lab's data pipeline are closely guarded trade secrets.
